Sri Gupt Ganga Tirtha

Sri Gupt Ganga Tirtha, an ancient Shiva temple, is nestled in Ishber, Srinagar, Jammu & Kashmir, near Ishwar Ashram and the scenic Dal Lake. The temple is known for its serene ambiance, sacred Shiva Linga, and its spiritual significance as a meditation haven.

The temple, a revered site of worship, holds immense importance in the spiritual and cultural history of the region. Mentioned in the Phatkisa Mahatya Khand of the Bhrigusamhita, it is regarded as a place where Ishwar resides as Shiva Shakti, accompanied by other gods. Kashmiri Pandits have historically performed ancestral rituals here, and the act of bathing in its sacred ponds is believed to cleanse sins and grant moksha, akin to the blessings of Kashi.

Mythological ties enrich its legacy, linking it to Danavaraj Bali’s Sri Charan Nami Yagna. During this sacred ritual, it is said that water from various tirthas flowed into this site, earning it the alternate names Vamana Tirtha and Gupt Tirtha. This historical and cultural lineage makes the Sri Gupt Ganga Tirtha a site of unparalleled reverence for devotees and scholars alike.

Historical Significance

The temple is mentioned in the Phatkisa Mahatya Khand of Bhrigusamhita. It is believed that bathing in the temple’s ponds and performing rituals here grants moksha, similar to that of Kashi.

The site is also associated with Danavaraj Bali, who conducted the Sri Charan Nami Yagna here, giving rise to the name Gupt Ganga.

Connections to Lakshman Joo & Abhinavagupta

From 1934 to 1935, Lakshman Joo lived above the village of Gupt Ganga, where Abhinavagupta resided nine centuries earlier. The area holds historical and spiritual importance, drawing scholars and devotees alike.

Sacred Springs

The temple complex features sacred springs, including one housing idols of Shiva, Parvati, and Ganesh. These springs are central to the temple’s spiritual and cultural practices.

Kashmir Shaiva Institute

Founded by Shaivacharya Lakshmanjoo Maharaj in 1969, the institute promoted the study of Kashmir Shaivism. Supported by Maharaja Karan Singh, it became a hub for spiritual education and lectures on Shaivism.
